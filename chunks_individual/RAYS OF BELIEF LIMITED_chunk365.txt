## **11. Psychotherapy and Counselling**

#### **Table 11: Description on diagnosis of ADHD**
|  Diagnostic Component  |  Description  |
|:---|:---|
| Clinical Assessment  | Healthcare providers gather detailed information from parents, teachers, and the individual about  the duration and impact of symptoms.  |
| Standardized Criteria  | Diagnoses are made based on criteria outlined in the DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of  Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition), requiring symptoms to be present for at least six months and to  significantly impair functioning.  |
| Rule Out Other  Conditions  | Other medical or psychological conditions that mimic ADHD symptoms, such as anxiety, learning  disabilities, or mood disorders, must be ruled out.  |
| Behavioral and  Developmental  Observation | Observing the child or adult in multiple settings provides valuable context for symptoms. ADHD  symptoms must be inconsistent with the individual’s developmental level. For example, while  some inattention or hyperactivity may be typical in children, ADHD behaviors are far more  pronounced. |
| Functional  Impairment  | Symptoms must result in significant functional impairment in at least one area of life—such as  school, work, or social relationships. The impairment must be observable and substantial, not just  a minor inconvenience.  |
