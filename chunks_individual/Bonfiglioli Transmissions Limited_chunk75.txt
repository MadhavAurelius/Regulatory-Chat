Co-engineering products involves multiple stages and can take up-to 30 months to complete. The extended gestation period inherent in co-engineering exposes us to material risks. For example, due to the limited visibility we have at the commencement of a long co-engineering process, we face the risk that the technology we are offering or utilising may become obsolete before the product reaches commercialisation, and any technological advancement or industry shift during the development cycle could render our solutions less competitive or outdated. Additionally, while we collaborate closely with customers to align with their product requirements, we may be unable to achieve intended results within the timeframe committed to the customer or at costs estimated at the outset. Changes in customer’s operational priorities, or market conditions during the co-engineering cycle could result in misalignment between the final product and customer needs, potentially leading to rejection of the product, contract modifications, or loss of revenue.
Co-engineering projects are also subject to macroeconomic conditions, supply chain disruptions, technical challenges, or unforeseen complications during prototyping, testing, or validation phases, which could extend development timelines and increase costs beyond initial projections. Such delays or cost escalations could adversely impact our profitability, damage customer relationships, and result in contractual penalties or loss of business.
